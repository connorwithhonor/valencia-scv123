The Problem Nobody Talks About

Your agent represents the buyer too?
Whose side are they really on?

California allows dual agency. One agent representing both the seller and the buyer on the same property. It's legal. But ask yourself: would you hire a lawyer who also represents the other side? Seven states have outlawed this practice entirely. Connor doesn't wait for California to catch up.

⚠️

No Dual Agency. Ever.

Connor does not represent buyers. Not on your listing. Not on any listing. If a buyer walks up to your open house without an agent, Connor will show them the property, walk them through every room, answer every question. But he will not represent them. No arm's length agreement. No referral to another agent. They find their own representation on their own.

🚫

No Referral Agreements On Your Listing

Connor will never be the catalyst for connecting an unrepresented buyer with another agent on your property. No referral agreement will be written. Nothing. Even the smallest thread of buyer referral carries a seed of potential dual agency. He will not touch it.

⚖️

No Favors Owed. No Obligations.

When your listing agent also works with buyers, they walk into your transaction owing people. Buyers they need to place. Colleagues whose offers they need to push. Agents expecting favors returned from past deals. That pressure lands on you. Your listing becomes the payback. Connor has no buyers. Owes no agent a favor. Nobody gets special treatment on your listing because he needs something on another deal.

The NAR Settlement Made This Cleaner

Since the NAR settlement, buyers now sign buyer broker agreements before touring homes. The buyer's agent commission is the buyer's business, not yours. This structural change makes the sellers-only model even more powerful. Connor was built for this era. Most agents are still adjusting.
